Bubble Acoustics

An air bubble detaches from an underwater nozzle 6 mm in internal diameter, captured at 4100 frames per second in Swinburne's Energy Transformation Laboratory. At the instant of detachment, the sudden change in interface topology causes a slight expansion or compression of the air now trapped in the newly-formed bubble, causing the bubble volume to "ring", emitting sound like a bell. Bubble-formation sounds account for much of the noise humans associate with water in motion. These sounds are being investigated for industrial and oceanic measurements.
